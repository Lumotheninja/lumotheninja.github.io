2017 Google Fake News Must Die Hackathon

We were top 2 out of 70 teams in Singapore

  1. Prompt
  2. Preparation
  3. Solution
  4. Results
  5. Learnings

Prompt

On 14 October 2017, me and my roommate Kai Xiang decided to join a Hackthon organized by NUS, SUTD, Media Literacy Council and Google. The aim? To kill fake news.

Goal: The organizers of this hackathon aim to reduce the impact of fake news – the rapid (and rampant) spread of inaccurate and fabricated misinformation and disinformation - by making it easy for users to quickly verify or check if a rumour or piece of information they receive is real.

I don’t have the exact details but we have like about 8-10 hours or so to come up with the solution.

Preparation

To be very honest, both me and Kai Xiang did very minimal preparation. Reason being:

  1. This is my first hackathon and we didn’t know what to expect.
  2. We were just aiming to gain experience from the Hackathon

Little did I know, most people would have already prepared datasets, built models and wrote websites for this hackathon. Heck, there was even a team of PHD students and researchers who took their active research project to analyze fake news to the Hackathon. We were severely underprepared and we didn’t know it.

Then again, I did read the blog post A plan for Spam by Paul Graham to prepare. I was at least prepared to write my own Naive-Bayes filter.

Solution

On the morning itself, we were first assigned to groups of media publishers to learn more about fake news. I think some teams didn’t attend this session and just started building their solution. We were assigned to a Mothership journalist who explained to us about fake news and their characteristics. We also learnt about the reason why publishers create fake news - usually ad revenue or political manipulation. Then we break for lunch.

After lunch, we are split into 5 groups to present our solution. Having limited time, me and Kai Xiang decided to go YOLO and we came up with a stupid solution - use fake news to fight fake news. The idea is simple - override the close and share button of a normal fake news site with a popup saying you have been tricked. Fight fire with fire. Use the virality of fake news to fight fake news itself.

Very quickly, Kai Xiang scraped the contents off a fake news website (an article about Morgan Freeman’s death) and I came out with the slides.

Results

When we presented our solution, the judge was very amused and we managed to get in the top 10 finalists. I think it was a refreshing break from all the machine learning/NLP/AI solutions that the other teams presented. However, the funny moment came during the on-stage presentation, where I started my presentation with “you either die a hero, or you live long enough to see yourself becoming the villain…”

In the end, we won 2nd place. We were given the honorable mention for our “cheeky and ballsy solution”. Not bad for a few hours work and I didn’t have to write my Naive Bayes filter.

Learnings

  1. Hackathons are meant to be like this. Lateral thinking instead of secretly preparing/tryharding beforehand.
  2. This strategy may not work everytime. In fact, in my next hackathon where I tried to pull this off again, it failed.
