2017 Startathon

The time when we created a bad buckle by design

  1. Prompt
  2. Preparation
  3. Solution
  4. Results
  5. Learnings

Prompt

We were only given the prompt one day before. The prompts are:

  1. How may we design an inspection device that can inspect crane-like structures autonomously and from a distance of not more than one meter for accurate inspections?
  2. How might we leverage on technology to encourage staff to voluntarily uphold safety in the workplace?
  3. How would you transform public health in Singapore?

Preparation

After our Google hackathon, we were thinking that perhaps we can replicate our success by using “innovation” as opposed to impressive technological breakthroughs to win. So, not much preparation once again. Also, the prompts are released only 1 day before so we cannot prepare much realistically.

I teamed up with Kai Xiang again and some juniors from SUTD (Lionell and friends)

Solution

When we got the prompt, we started brainstorming for ideas and we quickly settled on problem 2. Problem 1 seems too specific and problem 3 seems too generic. Plus, problem 2 seems to be one where we can “cheat” our way through with an innovative solution.

After discussing with the team, including auto-equip PPEs, detector gentries and IOT helmets, we settled on a low tech solution - PPEs that are easy to put on, but difficult to take off. We had a big laugh when we settled on the solution name - “a shitty buckle by design”, a play on our university motto. The design was meant to be uncomfortable so that users can overcome their bad habits of taking off their PPE. Here ia an example of bad design for good, which we tried to emulate.

Our first idea is a buckle that requires 2 hands to take off. Such buckle design would make it sublty difficult for workers to take off their helmets by pressing the buckle and removing it in one swiping motion. The keyword is subtly here, so we need to create a design that helps discourage behaviour of removing PPE yet making sure that it can be removed as required.

Once we got the concept and the idea across. Me and Kai Xiang quickly tried to prototype such a buckle. After some sewing, sawing and glueing, we managed to create a working prototype. It looks super funky and janky, but hey it works!

Results

In contrast to our Google hackathon, when we presented our solution, the judges were not impressed. In fact, they were much more impressed with the many IOT helmets that other groups are presenting. We returned empty handed this time, our “innovation solutions” hypothesis proven wrong.

Interestingly, there was a dude who talked to us afterwards wanting to manufacture our buckles. However, that didn’t happen after we emailed him twice and he didn’t reply.

Learnings

  1. My first overnight and hardware hackathon, it was really fun making stuff and having snacks to eat 24 hours, it was definitely an intense experience but worth it.
  2. My “innovation” hypothesis was proven wrong. Clearly, this hackathon is a technology hackathon, where impressive technologies trump over innovative solutions. I think the “innovation” hypothesis only works for idea hackathon where creative solutions are emphasized more.
